7 Reasons Why You Should Hike in Taiwan

If you have a short stay in Taiwan, have you ever wondered what you can do during your stay? If you google and think the only things you can do here are limited to visiting the National Palace of Museum and eating as many strange foods as you can at Shi-Lin Night Market with the crowd, you have come to the right place because you are about to experience the unique nature Taiwan can offer other than foods.

1. A wide variety of landscapes: from active volcanos to glacier traces

Taiwan not only has many mountains but also has a wide range of terrains. If you want to experience volcanos with smoke from craters, you got it.

You want to explore the lush jungles with water falls like Tarzan? Weâ€™ve got plenty. If you are an adrenaline junkie and want to spice up your stay, there are too many trails for you to choose.

Although Taiwan is a tropical island, you still can find glacier traces on some mountain summits above 3,000-meter sea level. Or, if you just want to have a short getaway to have a relaxing roaming, itâ€™s even closer than you could imagine.

2. Enjoy free hikes with super inviting and friendly local hiking communities. And yes, they speak some English.

As a result, itâ€™s not surprising to know that the hiking activities are popular here. We have many registered hiking clubs and they organize many free hiking trips on the weekends. Some hold events during weekdays.

For most of the hikes, you can just show up on time at the meeting points, meet new people or acquaintances, and enjoy the hike. If you opt to multi-day adventures, for a reasonable amount of money, you can just sign up and those hiking clubs will take care of mountain cabin or campsite applications, which is totally hassle free. (see 4 ways to enjoy hiking in Taiwan for details)

One of the unique things about those hiking clubs in Taiwan is the average age of the hikers is probably in mid 50 years old or even older, quite the opposite compared to other countries. Many of them are experienced hikers and even have finished 100 Three-thousanders in Taiwan and it's even difficult for younger people to keep up with their pace. They are also very welcoming. Some of them even prepare extra trail foods, like fresh fruit, iced drinks, etc., which is a big delight on the trail.

Some of the hikers speak English, probably not as fluent as native speakers, but at least you can communicate with them. Plus, hanging out with the local people is the best way to enhance your travel experience, so you donâ€™t have to worry that itâ€™s difficult to communicate with local hikers. When you are really exhausted, those guides will keep you company and make you feel you are well taken of.

3. Easy access transportations to the nature within 1 hour, especially good for people who have layover flights in Taipei.

Taipei probably has the clearest and safest transportation systems in the world, which makes it much easier to go hiking. Some trails are close to Taipei Metro Stations.

One of the most famous trails in Taipei is XianShan Hiking Trail, which allows you to see the whole Taipei City surrounded by hills, can be reached within 20 minutes on foot after you get off MRT station.

For trails outside of Taipei City, it is also easy to access them via trains or buses. Buses here are equipped with GPS, and passengers can check when the buses will come through their smartphones.

4. All kinds of trails to meet your hiking buds: from easy roaming to challenging ones.

Just like trails around the world, there are all kinds of trails labeled from being easy to very difficult. There are also many family friendly trails and small children can easily enjoy the hike. Some trails even allow you to enjoy the magnificent mountain and ocean views at the same time.

Some trails are very steep and require hikers to have a strong biceps because you have to grab the rope or tree roots to get yourself up or down. Some parts of the trails are on the narrow ridges and it can be very scary to walk on them. Some are barren rocks and you can see the deep valley down below. If you are not afraid of height, those trails will be great to explore.

Since the history of Taiwan can be traced back to hundreds of years ago, there were many main trails for porters to transport goods during that time. Although many are replaced by modern highways, you still can find the relics in the woods. Local governments maintain many historical trials. For people who love history, those historical trails are also great options to enjoy nature and history at the same time. (Add link)

5. You can hike all year round.

As long as there are no typhoons or heavy rains, you can go hiking whenever you are available. In summer, we like to hike at higher attitude to avoid the heat. Summer is also good for hiking Three-thousanders (link to hiking regulations in Taiwan), because you don't have to bring too much gear for the snow.

For people who hike on a regular basis, it's normal to hike in the rain. As long as you have proper clothing and gear to keep you warm and backpack dry, it might be refreshing to hike in drizzles, especially in hot weather.

For people who like water activities, it is very easy to find creeks, waterfalls, or lakes along the trails. You can also find several lakes above 3,000m/9842ft from the sea level in Taiwan. (link to lakes on the high mountains)

6. Taiwan is a heaven for outdoor adventure lovers.

There are more and more people embracing outdoor activities in Taiwan. Other than hiking, you can also go camping, scuba diving, surfing, rafting, river trekking, bouldering, kayaking, para-gliding, hot air ballooning, whale watching, etc.

Thus, you can find all kinds of registered clubs or companies to enjoy those adventures with qualified guides. Since Taiwan is small, itâ€™s even better and more convenient for adventure junkies to experience as many activities as possible within a short stay. (link to Other Outdoor Activities in Taiwan)

7. Wonderful foods for breakfast, lunch and post-hiking meals.

Last but not least, the food.
Many breakfast restaurants (it's literally written like that in Chinese) open as early as 5am or run business 24 hours, so you can stop by to buy foods for your breakfast and lunch. I usually buy Fan Tuan, which is made of sticky rice with some fillings like fried bread sticks and pork floss, or vegetarian ingredients. Fan Tuan is very fulfilling and easy to carry during the hike.

Or you can bring Ramen with you, and ask whether the guides can help you boil hot water. In Taiwan, Ramen is not just for broke students. Some expensive ones are with a small meat package inside. You can find all kinds of Ramen products at the supermarkets, luxuries, cheap, Taiwanese brands, or brands from South Asia, South Korea, or Japan.

If you are still not sure, there are convenience stores everywhere and you can get bread, Ramen, hot foods, snacks, drinks etc., for your trail food.

As to the post hiking meals, as long as you can reach any MRT station in Taipei, it's very easy to get to the famous food attractions. Furthermore, you don't have to go to ShiLin night market to enjoy the food, there are usually many restaurants near big MRT stations.

Most noodle restaurants serve beef noodle soups. There are also many vegetarian restaurants if you prefer.
As to my favorite post hiking meals, they are fried chicken and bubble tea. Those things are almost everywhere in Taiwan.
